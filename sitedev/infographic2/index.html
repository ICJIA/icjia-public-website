Promising practices in criminal justice: Assisting individuals with substance use disorders

There are currently an estimated 2.3 million individuals in U.S. prisons and jails, with 1.5 million meeting DSM-IV medical criteria for addiction (NIDA, 2014; CASA, 2010). Further, approximately 70 percent of state and 64 percent of federal inmates reported regular drug use prior to imprisonment; about 25 percent of violent offenders in state prisons committed their offenses under the influence of drugs (NIDA, 2014).

Spending-wise, 74 billion dollars was spent by federal, state, and local governments in 2005 for incarceration, court processing, and community-supervision of offenders with substance use disorders, and only 632 million spent on prevention and treatment (CASA, 2010). In addition, of incarcerated offenders with substance use disorders, only 11 percent actually received any treatment (CASA, 2010), proving it vital for the criminal justice system to incorporate avenues for substance buse treatment and diversion.

Regarding Illinois, drug offenses accounted for 99,953 arrests in 2015 (Illinois State Police, 2015). Moreover, Illinois is experiencing a widespread heroin and opioid epidemic. Heroin-related deaths rose from 149 to 681 between 2010 and 2014, with significant increases in Cook County (CDC Opioid Overdose State Data, 2016).

In response, criminal justice system, public health professionals, and legislators have tried to increase access to substance abuse treatment, inducing harm reduction tactics, and reduce influx of drugs in the community that contributes to abuse, addiction, and overdose.

This timeline explores criminal justice responses intended to deflect and/or divert individuals with substance use disorders from further involvement in the criminal justice system.

LAW ENFORCEMENT

Overdose Reversal

In Illinois, heroin-related deaths rose from 149 to 681 between 2010 and 2014, with significant increases in Cook County (CDC Opioid Overdose State Data, 2016). With the rise in heroin and opioid use and overdoses, Illinois passed the Heroin Crisis Act in 2014 [20 ILCS 301/5-23], requiring police officers and other first responders to carry Naloxone—an opioid antagonist used to counter effects of opioid overdose—to help prevent fatal overdoses. Naloxone (also known by the brand name Narcan) is a medicine that is administered by nasal spray or injection to reverse an opioid overdose. Naloxone cannot be used to get the same effects as heroin, and is not addictive. Emergency medical professionals have used it for decades. The Heroin Crisis Act also allows pharmacies to dispense naloxone to individuals without a prescription. More than 220 law enforcement agencies in 24 states carry Naloxone to help prevent fatal overdoses of opioid.

Pre-Arrest Diversion

In 2015, drug offenses accounted for 99,953 arrests in 2015 in Illinois (Illinois State Police, 2015). Pre-arrest diversion provides the ability to individuals with substance use disorders to receive help, prior to entry into the criminal justice system. The first program of this kind is the ANGEL program out of Gloucester, Massachusetts. Individuals with substance use disorders—without fear of arrest—can voluntarily turn in drugs and drug paraphernalia and is provided assistance in receiving substance abuse treatment (PAARI, 2016). Law enforcement agencies in Dixon, Fairview Heights, Lee County, Rolling Meadows, Whiteside County, Rock Falls, Morrison, and Sterling have partnered with Police Assisted Addiction and Recovery Initiative (PAARI). In 2015, the Dixon Police Department and the Lee County Sheriff’s Department created the Safe Passage initiative serving Lee, Livingston, and Whiteside Counties. In Lake County, Mundelein Police Department, in addition to others, created A Way Out initiative for pre-arrest diversion.

Post-Arrest, Pre-Booking Diversion

This type of drug diversion provides police with the opportunity to deflect low-level drug offenders from incarceration via diversion into community-based treatment services (Beckett, 2014). The first program of its kind, Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion (LEAD), comes out of Seattle, Washington, created October 2011. The goal of LEAD is to reduce recidivism and lower the monetary costs for the criminal justice system that are associate Red with conventional police practices (Collins, Lonczak, & Clifasefi, 2015). In order to accomplish this goal, LEAD provides its participants with resources ranging from case management and housing counsel to job-search support and drug and alcohol treatment.

OVERDOSE REVERSAL

One 2010 study of distribution programs in the country found that Naloxone reversed 10,171 overdoses (CDC, 2012). The Illinois Department of Alcohol and Substance Abuse (DASA) created 22 Drug Overdose Prevention Programs statewide that provide Narcan through more than 100 designated sites under the Heroin Crisis Act. Training events for its use have been held in Springfield, Chicago, Skokie, Lake County, Rockford, and Mt. Vernon, but though law enforcement agents were invited, it is unknown how many were trained. Further, the Chicago Recovery Alliance, an action organization for those interested in providing services, education, advocacy, and support for reducing drug-related harms, has one of the country’s largest Narcan programs. Between 2001 and 2006, the Alliance prescribed more than 3,500 vials of Narcan and received 319 reports of overdose reversals (Maxwell, Bigg, Stanczykiewicz, & Carlberg-Racich, 2006).

Post-Arrest, Pre-Booking Diversion

Researchers from the University of Washington provided a two year assessment and report of LEAD in Seattle. LEAD was able to provide a control group for comparison by arranging with Seattle Police Department (SPD) officers to ensure that offenders were either diverted directly to LEAD during a ‘greenlight shift’ or simply arrested and booked during a “redlight shift” (Collins et al., 2015). Offenders arrested during the redlight shift or in non-LEAD neighborhoods served as the control group. Using two outcome measures, one short-term and the other long-term, the researchers’ findings suggest that LEAD has a positive effect on participants (Collins et al., 2015). During the first six months of evaluation, 30 percent of LEAD participants were less likely to be arrested compared to the control group (Collins et al., 2015). Over the full course of the evaluation (October 2009-July 2014) indicate LEAD participants were 58 percent less likely to be arrested compared to the control group after their after entry evaluation (Collins et al., 2015). Overall, findings suggest that LEAD has both positive short- and long-term effects on its participants as it relates to arrests and felony charges (Collins et al., 2015). Other cities, such as Santa Fe, New Mexico and Albany, NY have begun to implement LEAD to help improve public health and safety.

Another program, Campaign Against Drug Abuse (CADA), organized police officers to conduct sweeps of certain neighborhoods. Low-level eligible offenders caught in such sweeps would be diverted from jail and taken to a location where a health worker would discuss treatment options with them (Goetz & Mitchell, 2006). Any individual who immediately agreed to treatment would be transported to an identified location to be accessed by treatment providers, while all others were cited and released (Goetz & Mitchell, 2006). CADA estimated that of the approximately 850 arrestees, most attended treatment (Goetz & Mitchell, 2006).

Additionally, San Francisco introduced Proposition 36 in 2000. This proposition required that all first- and second-time nonviolent drug offenders automatically be sent to treatment instead of jail (Fratello, 2006). In its first four years alone, Proposition 36 helped divert more than 140,000 individuals from incarceration to treatment. During the same period, state prison populations for drug possession decreased 32 percent, and construction of a new men’s prison was interrupted, thereby saving taxpayers’ money (Fratello, 2006).
